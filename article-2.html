Building Simple Multilingual Content Classification Models from Small Training Datasets

6 June 2021 · Posted by Sebastien

This article demonstrates a simple method that can be used to train content classification models with small training datasets that work across many languages that were not part of the training data. It attains an approximately 96% test set accuracy on the BBC news categorization dataset training on approximately 20 english news article examples per news category.

Furthermore, many of the classification errors are arguably not errors because the article reasonably fits both the label category and the predicted category. For example, an article about a technology business might be labelled as a business article but classified as a technology article by the model. This would be counted as an error in the model evaluation even though both categorizations are arguably correct. You can see a Jupyter notebook implementing the method here.

The final model can be used on 16 languages (Arabic, Chinese-simplified, Chinese-traditional, English, French, German, Italian, Japanese, Korean, Dutch, Polish, Portuguese, Spanish, Thai, Turkish, Russian) even though it was only trained with english examples. It does so by using transfer learning with the Multilingual Universal Sentence Encoder from Tensorflow Hub followed by a dropout regularization layer and a dense output layer. The Multilingual Universal Sentence Encoder (described in this paper) is a pre-trained Convolutional Neural Network that embeds text from 16 languages into a single shared 512 dimensional semantic embedding vector space. This semantic embedding space contains high level semantic features from which simple models that do not require much training data can be trained to perform various tasks. Building models to perform those same tasks equally well using raw text data as input would require much more training data.

You can find more examples of multilingual classification at the end of the example notebook.

To conclude, the main appeal of this content classification approach is that it is simple, requires a relatively small amount of training data and works on many languages even if they are not present in the training dataset.
